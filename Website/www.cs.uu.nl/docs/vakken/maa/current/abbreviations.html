Abbreviations

Probability

r.v.random variable A variable that assumes random values. Often we assume that a r.v. is coupled to a (discrete) p.f. or a (continuous) p.d.f.
r.v.s.random variables A (possibly infinite) set of random variables. Example: “Let Xi be a sequence of i.i.d. r.v.s.”
p.f.probability function A function from the range (set of possible values) of a discrete random variable to [0,1], such that its values sum to one. We write f(x) = P{ X=x }.
p.d.probability distribution The same.
p.d.f.probability density function A function from the range (set of possible values) of a continuous random variable to [0,1]. This function must integrate to one. We write ∫A f(x) = P{ x∈A }. It follows that f(x) = P{ X=x } = 0, for all x.
c.d.f.cumulative distribution function A function from the range (set of possible values) of a random variable to [0,1] that is monotone non-decreasing and continuous from the right. We write F(x) = P{ X≤x }.
d.f.distribution function The same.
c.f.characteristic function The c.f. χA of A is 1 on A and 0 elsewhere.
u.i.uniformly integrable A nice property on a set of random variables, sufficient for certain for results on convergence.
i.o.infinitely often Well, just what the man says: infinitely often!
a.s.almost surely A statement or property that holds with probability one.
a.c.almost certainly The same.
a.e.almost everywhere A statement or property that holds everywhere except on a subset of measure zero.
i.d.infinitely divisible A c.d.f. is infinitely divisible if its c.f. is the nth power of a c.f. fn, for every n.
i.i.d.independent and identically distributed A set of random variables is i.i.d. if they are coupled to the same d.f. and are assumed to be independent.
CLTcentral limit theorem (Lindeberg-Levy version): the average of a large number of i.i.d. r.v.s. approximates the normal distribution. How fast? One rate of convergence is given by Berry-Esséen. (The CLT implies the WLLN.)
WLLNweak law of large numbers With arbitrary high probability, the average of a large enough number of i.i.d. r.v.s. lies arbitrary close to the mean: Xn →P X. More concretely: for every small ε>0, the probability that the average of a large number of dice throws differs less than ε from 3.5 converges to 1.
SLLNstrong law of large numbers The average of a large number of i.i.d. r.v.s. approximates the mean a.s.: Xn →a.s. X. More concretely: if you throw a dice, then the average will converge to 3.5 with probability 1. (The last statement cannot be made with WLLN.)
LILlaw of the iterated logarithm Describes the magnitude of the fluctuations of a random walk.
m.g.f.moment generating function Another way to define the p.d. of a random variable, involving powers of e.
→dconvergence in distribution Pointwise converge of associated p.d.f.'s .
→Pconvergence in probability Probability that terms remain within any pre-specified level of accuracy from the limit r.v. tends to one.
→a.s.convergence almost surely Convergence with probability one.
→L1convergence in mean Expected difference between term and limit r.v.s. tends to zero.
